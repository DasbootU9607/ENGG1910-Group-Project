ENGG1910 Course Project

AI-Powered Misinformation Risk Assistance for Social Media Users

A reproducible NLP system that ranks political claims by misinformation risk and explains why a claim may deserve verification.

Code Paper PDF Results

Abstract

This project frames misinformation detection as user-facing risk assistance rather than final truth judgment. We evaluate on LIAR, a public benchmark of 12.8K political claims labelled by fact-checkers, and map six truthfulness labels into a binary risk task. Under a fair text-only protocol, BERT-base reaches 0.623 F1 and 0.697 ROC-AUC, slightly ahead of a text-only TF-IDF logistic regression baseline. A metadata-enhanced TF-IDF model reaches 0.719 F1 and 0.830 ROC-AUC when speaker-history information is allowed.

Method

1

Input

Claim text, subject, context, speaker metadata, and optional social-media signals.

2

Features

TF-IDF n-grams, speaker history, warning terms, source and repost indicators.

3

Models

Text-only TF-IDF, text-only BERT-base, and a separate metadata-enhanced model.

4

Output

Risk score, Low/Medium/High level, and human-readable explanation.

Analysis

Ethics and Limitations

The assistant should support verification, not censorship. False positives may unfairly reduce trust in legitimate claims, while false negatives may create over-confidence. A deployed system would need privacy-preserving metadata handling, multilingual evaluation, stronger source reliability signals, and real repost-network analysis.
